# 5.1 Flow Variability

## 5.1.1 Introduction
In previous chapters, variability was mainly associated with:
- processing times,
- machine breakdowns,
- repair times.

In this chapter, the focus shifts to **flow variability**, i.e. variability generated by:
- interactions between stations,
- merging and splitting of flows,
- propagation of variability along the production line.

Flow variability explains why even perfectly reliable machines may experience congestion.

---

## 5.1.2 Definition of Flow Variability
Flow variability refers to **variability in arrival processes** generated internally by the system.

Even if:
- external arrivals are regular,
- processing times are deterministic,

internal flows can still be highly variable due to:
- queues,
- blocking,
- synchronization effects.

---

## 5.1.3 Variability Propagation
Variability propagates downstream:

- a variable departure process from one workstation becomes
- a variable arrival process for the next workstation.

This phenomenon is called **variability amplification**.

Key consequence:
> downstream stations often experience **higher variability** than upstream stations.

---

## 5.1.4 Departure Process Variability

Consider a workstation with:
- arrival variability C_A²,
- processing time variability C_S²,
- utilization U.

The squared coefficient of variation of the **departure process** is approximated by:

```
C_D² ≈ (1 − U²) · C_A² + U² · C_S²
```

This formula shows that:
- at low utilization, departures resemble arrivals,
- at high utilization, departures resemble processing times.

---

## 5.1.5 Interpretation of the Departure Formula
- If U → 0:
```
C_D² ≈ C_A²
```
- If U → 1:
```
C_D² ≈ C_S²
```

Thus:
- low utilization filters variability,
- high utilization transfers variability.

---

## 5.1.6 Variability at the Next Workstation
For a downstream workstation:
```
C_A,downstream² = C_D,upstream²
```

This means:
- arrival variability is **endogenous**,
- controlling variability at one station affects the entire line.

---

## 5.1.7 Example: Two Workstations in Series

### Given
- External arrivals: deterministic → C_A² = 0
- Processing times exponential at WS1 → C_S² = 1
- Utilization at WS1: U = 0.9

Departure variability from WS1:
```
C_D² ≈ (1 − 0.9²)·0 + 0.9²·1 = 0.81
```

Arrival variability at WS2:
```
C_A² = 0.81
```

Even though arrivals were deterministic at the system entrance, WS2 receives **highly variable flow**.

---

## 5.1.8 Impact on Queueing Performance
Using the VUT equation:
```
CTQ ≈ (C_A² + C_S²)/2 · U/(1 − U) · E[TS]
```

An increase in arrival variability:
- increases queue cycle time,
- increases WIP,
- worsens overall system performance.

---

## 5.1.9 Flow Variability vs Natural Variability
- Natural variability originates inside the process.
- Flow variability originates from **system interactions**.

Flow variability can dominate natural variability in long production lines.

---

## 5.1.10 Managerial Implications
To reduce flow variability:
- avoid very high utilizations,
- introduce buffers strategically,
- synchronize flows carefully,
- reduce processing time variability upstream.

Local decisions affect global performance.

---

## 5.1.11 Key Takeaways
- Flow variability is internally generated.
- Variability propagates and amplifies downstream.
- High utilization transfers variability.
- Controlling variability upstream benefits the entire system.
